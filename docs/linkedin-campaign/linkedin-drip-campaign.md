# Campaign Summary

## Campaign Objective

Show how vulnerability management discipline transfers into application security by telling a credible, story-first case study about turning signals into validated, owner-assigned remediation work.

## Target Audience

Primary:
- AppSec professionals
- vulnerability management leaders
- DevSecOps engineers
- security engineering managers
- cloud security and application security teams

Secondary:
- hiring managers
- recruiters
- newer professionals who want a practical model for building and explaining security projects

## Core Positioning Statement

This project demonstrates how vulnerability management discipline can be applied to application security by turning findings into owned, validated, evidence-backed remediation work.

## Three-Post Narrative Arc

1. The Program: establish the controlled lab, the Azure/Terraform foundation, and the AppSec workflow.
2. The Finding: show how a real signal becomes validated, prioritized, and assigned remediation work.
3. The Proof: show how response minimization, tests, re-testing, and evidence support closure.

## Recommended Publishing Schedule

- Day 1: Post 1
- Day 4: Post 2
- Day 8: Post 3

## Recommended General Posting Windows

- Morning window: 8:00 AM to 10:00 AM ET
- Lunch window: 11:30 AM to 1:00 PM ET
- Early evening window: 4:30 PM to 6:00 PM ET

These windows are a practical starting point, not a guarantee of engagement.

## Campaign Voice

The voice should feel like a confident security professional telling a real story, not a marketing writer or a résumé summary. It should be technically precise, conversational, curious, and slightly dramatic without becoming theatrical. The tone should invite discussion from peers while still being accessible to people newer to the field.

## Thumbnail Design System

- Dark navy, charcoal, or near-black backgrounds
- Cyan as the primary trust and clarity accent
- Amber and red for risk states
- Green or cyan for validated closure
- Bold sans-serif typography
- Minimal text
- Clean cards, pipelines, API motifs, and subtle grid structure
- No stock photography
- No tool-logo collage
- No hashtags or URLs on the image

## Selected Thumbnail Headlines

- Post 1: `FIVE ISSUES. ONE APP.`
- Post 2: `A FINDING IS NOT A FIX`
- Post 3: `FIXED IS NOT ENOUGH`

## Call-to-Action Strategy

Each post should end with an open, conversation-oriented question. The goal is to invite practitioners to compare workflows, question assumptions, and share what they would add or change. Examples include:
- What would you add first?
- What do you need before you call a finding real work?
- What do you require before you close a fix?
- What did I miss?
- How would you make this stronger?

## Engagement Strategy

- Reply quickly to early comments so the post feels like a live conversation.
- Ask follow-up questions when people add thoughtful context.
- Use practitioner-facing language first, recruiter-facing signal second.
- Highlight lessons learned instead of claiming perfection.
- Keep the discussion grounded in evidence, ownership, and validation.

## Five Suggested Responses to Comments

1. “That’s a great point. I was trying to keep the story focused on the workflow, but I agree the owner handoff is where a lot of programs get stuck.”
2. “Exactly. The scanner output matters, but the real work starts when someone has to explain and own the risk.”
3. “I like that addition. It would make the closure path even stronger and closer to how a real team would operate.”
4. “That’s the kind of nuance I was hoping to surface here. The fix is only useful if someone can prove it stayed fixed.”
5. “Good catch. I left that out intentionally to keep the post readable, but it’s an important piece of the broader AppSec picture.”

## Publication Checklist

- Confirm the repository link is correct in all three posts
- Confirm there are exactly three posts
- Confirm each post uses the approved discussion-oriented CTA style
- Confirm the campaign is framed as a controlled portfolio lab
- Confirm the tone is story-first and not recruiter-first
- Confirm each thumbnail headline matches its post
- Confirm the assets are readable on mobile

## Technical Accuracy Checklist

- Verify every technical claim against repository evidence
- Distinguish the controlled lab from production experience
- Avoid overstating any single scanner or validation method
- Do not imply complete security coverage
- Do not claim a vulnerability is eliminated without before, fix, and re-test evidence
- Keep OWASP mapping aligned to the repository’s own reports and tickets

## Claims Intentionally Avoided

- Production incident claims
- Enterprise implementation claims
- Business impact claims
- Cost or time savings claims
- Claims that the lab is a substitute for full AppSec assurance
- Claims that one scanner proves the absence of all vulnerabilities
- Claims that the lab represents professional production experience
